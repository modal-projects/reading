## Overview

This spike is narrower than a general tour of evolutionary algorithms. The question it tries to answer
is: **where does population-based search actually pay off for Modal right now, and what does the
platform need to provide to make it cheap?** The same primitive — sample a population of perturbed
candidates, score them cheaply in parallel, move the parent toward the good ones — keeps showing up
on three very different surfaces:

- **Training.** EGGROLL turns evolution strategies into something that behaves like batched LoRA
  inference, reaches ~100x the throughput of naive ES at billion-parameter scale, and hits or beats
  GRPO on RWKV-7 reasoning tasks.
- **Hyperparameter tuning.** PBT treats a fleet of concurrent training runs as a population and
  evolves both weights and hyperparameters inside the same job budget.
- **Scheduling.** The honey-bee model treats servers as workers advertising recent reward
  (dancelets) and lets them re-specialize without a central coordinator.

The interesting thing is not that these algorithms exist. It is that they all have the same shape,
and that shape fits Modal's existing platform primitives more cleanly than most of the systems they
compete with.

## Modal context

- The current RL-on-Modal story is GRPO-shaped (TRL, verl, the GRPO tutorial). EGGROLL is the first
  credible pitch that something very different on the algorithm side can ride the same substrate
  without asking for low-latency weight sync.
- The existing **massively parallel hyperparameter tuning** example is already the right shape for
  PBT; the upgrade is mostly in exploit/explore bookkeeping and shared checkpoints, not in core
  platform capability.
- **Modal Flash 0→1 autoscaling** and the scheduler's slot allocation face a decentralized
  assignment problem under shifting, heterogeneous demand. The honey-bee algorithm does not drop in,
  but it is a cleaner baseline to argue against than another ad-hoc heuristic.

## Key themes

- Population-based search wins on platforms where **inference is cheap and elastic**, not where
  individual gradient steps are cheap. That describes Modal more than it describes most training
  clusters.
- The modern ES comeback is not "no gradients", it is **"gradient-like updates over a batched
  inference forward pass"**. EGGROLL's rank-r perturbations make this explicit.
- PBT blurs the line between hyperparameter search and training: the schedules it discovers
  (learning rate, entropy coefficient) matter more than the static configs grid sweeps find.
- Swarm / nature-inspired scheduling is most useful as a **framing**, not as a drop-in algorithm —
  it forces a clear separation between the local signal each worker sees and the global property
  (load balance, revenue, throughput) the fleet is supposed to produce.
- Across all three, the thing that gets cheaper is **population size**, not per-member quality. The
  platform question is how cheaply Modal can run N-way parallel inference with occasional
  synchronization, not how fast a single worker runs.

## Suggested reading order

1. Start with **Lil'Log: Evolution Strategies** for vocabulary — simple Gaussian ES, CMA-ES, NES,
   OpenAI ES, and PBT all live in one post, which is what makes the rest of the reading list
   legible.
2. Read **Evolution Strategies at the Hyperscale (EGGROLL)** for the concrete reason this is
   worth looking at now: rank-r perturbations, batched-inference throughput, and the head-to-head
   with GRPO on RWKV-7.
3. Read **Population Based Training of Neural Networks** for the hyperparameter-tuning side. It is
   the cleanest public description of "treat a training fleet as a population" and is the natural
   follow-up to Modal's existing hyperparameter-tuning example.
4. Finish with **A Honey Bee Algorithm for Internet Server Colony Optimization** to flip the
   framing from "evolve models" to "evolve allocations", and evaluate how much of its framing
   maps onto Flash-shaped decentralized autoscaling.
