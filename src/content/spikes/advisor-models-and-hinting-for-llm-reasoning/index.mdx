## Overview

The training team is in two places at once. On one side, the RL harness is being
productionized for customers who want verifiable-reward post-training against
their own evals. On the other, the Lovable workstream has a concrete
advisor/executor loop where the next real step is to train a small advisor so
that paired evals actually move. These two efforts converge on the same
narrow question: **what does it take to make RL training signal survive once the
executor is frozen, the reward is sparse, and the trajectories are long?**

The answer emerging across recent work is that the useful unit of training is
the advisor or hint distribution, not the executor. An advisor model is small,
cheap to roll out, and can be trained with RL against a frozen frontier
executor. Hints reshape the rollout distribution instead of the reward, which
sidesteps the classic failure modes of long-CoT RL - length runaway, advantage
collapse, reward hacking - while keeping the terminal verifier honest.

The Yeo et al. paper sets the stage: long CoT emerges from RL, but only when
reward shaping stabilizes it. The remaining sources are the operating manual for
doing that shaping through an advisor rather than through the executor.

## Context

- **Frozen executors are the norm.** Customers increasingly train against black-box
  frontier models (via API) or premium open checkpoints they are unwilling to
  overwrite; the trainable surface is the small advisor or hint model.
- **Sparse terminal rewards are the default** for verifiable-reward post-training.
  GRPO's relative-advantage signal collapses when every rollout in a group gets
  the same reward, which is exactly what happens on hard reasoning tasks before
  a run has warmed up.
- **Long-CoT trajectories are expensive per step.** That makes advisor-style
  partitioning (cheap hints, expensive executor) not just a training
  abstraction but a real inference-economics lever.
- The Modal-side concern is matching this shape with the RL harness and
  training gym: expose verifier hooks, per-rollout observability, and
  hint-distribution controls at the right layer so customers can run these
  recipes without rebuilding scaffolding.

## Key themes

- **Advisor, not executor, is the trainable surface.** Asawa et al. make this
  explicit: RL updates on a small advisor model are what move downstream
  performance of a frozen frontier executor.
- **Hints reshape the rollout distribution, not the reward.** SAGE keeps the
  terminal verifier reward unchanged and injects self-generated hints during
  rollouts to stop GRPO advantages from collapsing under sparse rewards. This
  is a different lever than reward shaping, and the two are complementary.
- **Step-level uncertainty matters more than trajectory-level outcomes.**
  SMART's score-based selective scaffolding and the long-CoT paper's framing of
  error correction as a latent-but-incentivizable capability both point away
  from pure outcome-only reward toward some form of step-level signal.
- **Hints are an inference-economics primitive too.** LLM Shepherding shows
  that the same cheap-advisor/expensive-executor partition that trains well
  also serves cheaply: 42-94% inference cost reduction versus full LLM
  generation at matched accuracy.
- **Reward shaping is still the backstop.** None of the hint-based recipes
  remove the need for a well-shaped reward. Yeo et al. is the reminder that if
  the underlying RL loop does not have CoT length and verifiable reward under
  control, no amount of advisor cleverness fixes it.

## Suggested reading order

1. Start with **Yeo et al., Demystifying Long CoT** to set the RL-side ground
   truth: what reward shaping actually buys you when CoT length is the
   emergent variable, and what SFT vs. pure-RL really trade off.
2. Read **Asawa et al., Advisor Models** next. It is the clearest statement of
   "the advisor is the trainable surface" and the paper most directly aligned
   with the Lovable workstream's shape.
3. Read **SAGE (Liao et al.)** for the concrete GRPO fix. It is the most
   actionable recipe in the set for an RL harness: same verifier, hint-augmented
   rollouts, no-hint policy at test time.
4. Read **SMART (Yi et al.)** for the step-level intervention angle. It is the
   closest external analogue to fine-grained credit assignment over reasoning
   steps and a useful mirror for the eval-and-rubric work already underway.
5. Finish with **LLM Shepherding (Dong et al.)** to flip the framing to
   inference economics. The same partition that makes advisor RL work in
   training is also what makes mixed SLM+LLM serving measurably cheaper.
